\begin{abstract}
\noindent
\textbf{Where we are (2026-07-14, after the fixes).} Two real, independent bugs explain the
catastrophes of the past week; both are now fixed and under test (movcav16):

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Bug I --- PISM's ocean forcing was surface water.} The \code{-ocean th} route averaged
FESOM level indices 1--20 = \textbf{0--410\,m including the summer surface}: PISM received ice-base
water of up to \textbf{$+6.5^\circ$C} even from a healthy ocean, melted accordingly
($\sim$30\,m/yr), and shed \textbf{24\% of its floating area per decade} --- so every mesh-change
leg had to absorb a catastrophic geometry increment. FESOM's \emph{own} cavity melt in the same
healthy runs is sane: \textbf{2.2\,m/yr mean, 0.72 median}. Fix: PISM now consumes FESOM's own melt
directly (\code{-ocean given}, the DIRECT route; esm\_tools \code{93a207ee}).

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Bug II --- the offline feom exchange weights were in the wrong node ordering.} FESOM
exchanges coupling fields in \emph{partition (my\_list/rank) order}; the offline weight engine
(2026-06-17) built them in \emph{nod2d.out order}. Result: every A096$\leftrightarrow$feom exchange
in every submesh leg was \textbf{geographically tile-translocated} --- the atmosphere was coupled to
a jigsaw ocean (Fig.~\ref{fig:jigsaw}): OIFS saw Weddell ice fraction \textbf{0.04} while FESOM had
\textbf{0.95}; global sea-ice death followed within months. Fix: permute to runtime ordering +
a mandatory weight validator that aborts the leg on ordering mismatch (\code{c21fe7c2}).

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Four earlier root-cause claims are falsified} and struck through below. Sections whose
measurements were made on jigsaw-coupled runs are marked \textbf{TAINTED} and will be re-measured
under movcav16.
\end{abstract}

\section{The two real bugs (2026-07-14)}\label{sec:bugs}

\subsection{Bug I: PISM was fed surface water as ice-base forcing}\label{sec:bug1}

\code{coupling\_ocean2pism.functions} built PISM's \code{-ocean th} forcing as a vertical mean over
FESOM level \emph{indices} 1--20 (\code{-sellevidx,1/20}) = \textbf{0--410\,m, surface included};
the correct \code{-sellevel,150/600} sat in dead code behind \code{if [ 0 -eq 1 ]}. Measured
consequence in the \emph{healthy} chain (movcav8): \code{theta\_ocean} fed to PISM had mean
$-0.43^\circ$C and \textbf{max $+6.53^\circ$C} (realistic ice-base: $\approx -1.9$). PISM's
three-equation scheme melted from that at $\sim$30\,m/yr and collapsed the shelves: $-24\%$
floating area per decade, 1399 nodes losing their ice \emph{entirely} per 10-yr leg, Ross front
$-440$\,km (Fig.~\ref{fig:pism}). Meanwhile \textbf{FESOM's own melt} in those same healthy runs:
\textbf{2.2\,m/yr mean, 0.72 median, deep-cavity 2.6} --- sane.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Fix (\code{93a207ee}):} the DIRECT route. PISM consumes FESOM's own cavity fluxes via
\code{-ocean given}: \code{fw}$\to$\code{shelfbmassflux} ($\times 1000$; sign preserved --- FESOM
melt is $fw<0$ and PISM's \code{shelfbmassflux} is ``positive if ice is freezing on''),
\code{sst}(level 1 = ice base under the virtual-column convention)$\to$\code{shelfbtemp}. One model
owns the ice--ocean interface. The \code{-ocean given} machinery existed all along; it silently
fell back to \code{th} because its input is a FESOM1.x-era file (\code{EXP.year.pism.nc}) that
FESOM2 never writes --- \code{fesom2ice} now synthesizes it from the standard monthly streams. The
150--600\,m band is also enabled as a safety for anyone still on \code{th}.

\subsection{Bug II: the exchange weights scrambled the coupled ocean}\label{sec:bug2}

\begin{figure}[h]\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{spp/sst_scramble_global.png}
\caption{\textbf{The picture that settles it.} January (month 1) sea-surface temperature as OIFS
holds it. Top: movcav4, pool weights --- textbook. Bottom: movcav15, offline-engine weights ---
the ocean is a \emph{tile-translocated jigsaw}: tropical blocks at $55^\circ$S, polar blocks on the
equator, a straight seam at lon 180 where the mesh node ordering breaks.}\label{fig:jigsaw}
\end{figure}

FESOM registers its OASIS partition rank-contiguously (Apple partition, \code{cpl\_driver.F90}):
the exchange field is the concatenation of each rank's owned nodes in \code{my\_list} order ---
\textbf{not} \code{nod2d.out} order (median displacement between the two: $58^\circ$ of latitude).
The offline weight engine introduced on 2026-06-17 built the feom geometry in nod2d order. The
weights were validated structurally (dims, layout) but never \emph{applied to a field} --- and at
runtime every exchange was tile-translocated. The canonical pool weights are correct because they
were \emph{harvested from a real coupled run} (hence the per-nproc pool directories).

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Evidence} (all on movcav15 itself): OIFS ci = 0.04 vs FESOM a\_ice = 0.95 in month 1;
1920 tropical cells $<5^\circ$C and 746 polar cells $>15^\circ$C in OIFS's January SST (0 and 0 in
movcav4); offline application of the exact staged weights to the exact FESOM ice field reproduces
OIFS's received 0.04; global sea-ice death with \textbf{no winter regrowth} in both hemispheres;
cavity $+1.25$\,K, melt 28\,m/yr \emph{regardless of initial state} --- the heat arrived through the
scrambled exchange every timestep. Every submesh leg since June carried this (masked there by the
day-4 crashes); the healthy chunk-1 runs (movcav4/7/8) used pool weights.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Fix (\code{c21fe7c2}, option B1 --- in the coupling layer, ocp-tool untouched):}
\code{permute\_feom\_to\_runtime.py} reorders \code{feom.*} in grids/masks/areas to runtime order
(from the submesh's own \code{dist\_<nproc>/rpart.out}, verified identical to the \code{my\_list}
concatenation FESOM reads); \code{validate\_feom\_weights.py} then applies every weight file to the
runtime-ordered node latitudes and \textbf{aborts the leg} (couple\_fail) if the median error
exceeds $1^\circ$ (nod2d-ordered weights fail at 42--58$^\circ$; correct ones pass at
$\le 0.025^\circ$). The validator would have caught the bug the day the engine was introduced.
Round-trip tested on compute; the pooled chunk-1 artifacts are fixed in place (backups kept).

\subsection{How the two bugs interlocked}

Bug I collapsed PISM even in healthy runs $\Rightarrow$ monstrous per-leg geometry increments.
Bug II poisoned the runs used to diagnose Bug I: measured on jigsaw-coupled movcav15, ``FESOM's own
melt'' appeared to be 28\,m/yr, which falsely disqualified the DIRECT fix and sent the
investigation into the cavity-water/SPP dead end. \textbf{Measurements from a corrupted run are
worse than no measurements.}

\section{Blocker I --- OIFS step-0 coastline crash (RESOLVED)}

A floating-point NaN struck exactly the coastline cells whose land--sea type flipped. \code{ocp-tool}
half-converted a flipped cell (only mask + soil type), OIFS re-ingested the inconsistency from the
regenerated \code{ICMGG} and NaN'd in moist physics. Fixed by a masked nearest-neighbour rebuild of
each flipped cell's full column, plus the \code{ICMGG} re-ingest (\code{PUTALLFLDS\_M} in
\code{reresf\_part2.F90}). A recurring \code{voskin} floating-overflow variant shows up
state-dependently and was traced to the native-pipeline \code{ICMGG} coastal lsm.

\subsection{What is actually wrong: the dynamics seam}\label{sec:seam}

The remap produces an internally inconsistent state: on the \textbf{unchanged nodes (98\%)} it
reproduces the evolved restart \textbf{bit-exactly} (a fully developed, balanced dynamic state:
$\mathbf{u}$, $\eta$, AB history); on the \textbf{changed nodes} it substitutes \emph{patched} values.
The two regions are mutually incompatible, and the \textbf{seam between them} is the instability.
Zeroing the dynamics globally removes the crash (the ocean re-derives its own balanced flow from
rest) --- which is exactly why all five fill strategies failed: each varied the \emph{patch} while
leaving the \emph{seam}. Zero-dynamics is not a production fix: discarding the ocean's circulation
every leg is unacceptable.

\subsection{This is not good news}\label{sec:sober}

It is tempting to read the numbers optimistically: only 17 of 1399 fully-opened nodes rang; the ocean
absorbed $\sim$99\%; the CFLz transient peaked and \emph{decayed}. All true, and it does mean the
remap machinery is performing better than a ``crashed at day 15'' headline suggests.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{But the honest reading is negative.} The run still crashed. What we have learned is that
\textbf{our ocean reacts violently to ice-shelf and cavity changes mid-run}: a handful of columns
where the ice vanishes outright is enough to kill a leg. A coupled setup in which the ice sheet is
\emph{allowed} to change its cavity every 10 years cannot be one leg's worth of extreme nodes away
from failure. \textbf{Absorbing 99\% of the change is not a passing grade when the remaining 1\% is
fatal.}

\section{Bugs found and fixed along the way}

\subsection{In the remap}

\textbf{Unbounded extrapolation below the old seabed} (esm\_tools \code{eef4f6db}). The first attempt
at the increment leg died at \textbf{mstep 1}: the remapped restart contained $-45.02^\circ$C, a value
present \emph{nowhere} in the source (wet range $[-2.32, 41.72]$, dry cells $0.0$). The remap
\emph{manufactured} it. The column shows the mechanism:
$1.22, -0.99, -3.19, -5.39, -8.69, -13.09, -18.6, -25.2, -34.01, \mathbf{-45.02}$ --- smooth and
accelerating. For columns that get \emph{deeper}, the remap fitted a straight line through the last
two old levels and continued it downward with no clamp. Fixed by taking such water from the nearest
old node actually wet at that depth. \textbf{Never hit before} because every previous remap went
mother\,$\to$\,submesh (a strict \emph{subset}); ice \emph{retreating} grows the mesh and exercises
this path for the first time.

\medskip\noindent
Also: sub-ice \code{ssh}/\code{hbar} zeroing (\code{b17fb5c8}); a \textbf{size guard} so the remap
refuses a restart that does not match the old mesh, instead of silently writing garbage
(\code{3b44a704}).

\subsection{In the coupling plumbing}

Making chunk~1 a \emph{submesh} leg pushed a submesh leg through \code{couple\_out} for the first
time. Every previous run either ran chunk~1 on the \emph{full mesh} (where these paths are trivially
correct) or died early in chunk~3 (before reaching the end of a leg). That exposed:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{OASIS \code{GSSPOS} abort at $\sim$day 300.} Its conservation correction is built from
      the summed source/target ratio; when the summed target residual is $\approx 0$ while the source
      residual is small-but-nonzero, it explodes. Only the heat-to-ice flux (\code{A\_Q\_ice
      $\to$ heat\_ico}) used it. Fixed: new \code{gauswgt\_gsmart} transform (\code{3e94d4da}).
\item \textbf{The namcouple \code{feom} dimension had NEVER been patched, on any leg.}
      \code{fix\_namcouple\_feom\_dim} read \code{\$\{MAXMESH\_DIR\_fesom\}}, which is assigned
      \emph{inside} \code{build\_submesh} --- a different subjob's shell. Always unset $\Rightarrow$
      guard refused $\Rightarrow$ silent no-op. Fixed (\code{3cf29e9e}).
\item \textbf{\code{fesom2ice} loaded the static full mesh} while FESOM's output is on the submesh
      (broadcast error) (\code{63cfcc91}).
\item \textbf{\code{previous\_submesh} is read but never created} --- anywhere. Every leg from chunk~5
      on would have remapped against the wrong mesh (\code{3b44a704}).
\item \textbf{A failed \code{couple\_in} did not stop the workflow.} The coupling function is
      backgrounded, the observe job is not its parent (so it can never see the exit code), and
      \code{observe.py} returns an \emph{empty} error list for every non-compute job. The next leg
      started on an un-remapped restart. Fixed with a \code{.coupling\_failed} sentinel
      (\code{3b44a704}).
\end{itemize}

\noindent
\textbf{The pattern:} the guards were right, and the plumbing around them defeated them.
\code{remap\_oasis\_restart} correctly refused a mis-sized restart --- and the run proceeded anyway.

\section{Method note}

Three root causes were asserted with more confidence than the evidence supported, and all three were
later falsified by a control that was cheap and should have come \emph{first}:

\begin{enumerate}
\item ``geostrophic imbalance'' --- killed by the \textbf{cold start} (is it the geometry or our
      state?);
\item ``the sub-ice ssh BC'' --- killed by simply \textbf{running the fix};
\item ``the increment is gentle ($\approx$22\,m)'' --- killed by \textbf{plotting the distribution}
      instead of trusting a mean over a domain that is 98\% unchanged.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
The third is the sharpest: the 22\,m figure was computed correctly and reported honestly, and it was
still misleading enough to steer the strategy for a day. \textbf{Two figures did in ten minutes what
a summary statistic obscured for a day.}
