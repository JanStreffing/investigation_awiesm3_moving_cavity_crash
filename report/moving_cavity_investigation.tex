\begin{abstract}
\noindent
\textbf{Where we are.} PISM is in massive disequilibrium and is shedding its ice shelves: it loses
\textbf{24\% of its floating area per decade}, \textbf{1399 mesh nodes lose their ice entirely in a
single 10-yr leg}, and the Ross front retreats $\approx$440~km. Our ocean \textbf{reacts violently}
to that: it absorbs $\sim$99\% of the change, but the remaining 1\% (columns where the ice vanishes
outright) is enough to kill the leg. \textbf{This is not good news.} Absorbing 99\% is not a passing
grade when the rest is fatal.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Two root causes we published and later falsified} are struck through below. The current
understanding: the cavity \emph{geometry} is fine (a cold start on it is stable at the production
timestep); the killer is the \emph{seam} in our remapped restart, where patched values at the changed
nodes meet a fully evolved dynamical state everywhere else.
\end{abstract}

\section{Blocker I --- OIFS step-0 coastline crash (RESOLVED)}

A floating-point NaN struck exactly the coastline cells whose land--sea type flipped. \code{ocp-tool}
half-converted a flipped cell (only mask + soil type), OIFS re-ingested the inconsistency from the
regenerated \code{ICMGG} and NaN'd in moist physics. Fixed by a masked nearest-neighbour rebuild of
each flipped cell's full column, plus the \code{ICMGG} re-ingest (\code{PUTALLFLDS\_M} in
\code{reresf\_part2.F90}). A recurring \code{voskin} floating-overflow variant shows up
state-dependently and was traced to the native-pipeline \code{ICMGG} coastal lsm.

\textbf{\textcolor{red}{FALSIFIED \#1 --- ``a geostrophically-unbalanced remapped state''.}}

\subsection{What is actually wrong: the dynamics seam}\label{sec:seam}

The remap produces an internally inconsistent state: on the \textbf{unchanged nodes (98\%)} it
reproduces the evolved restart \textbf{bit-exactly} (a fully developed, balanced dynamic state:
$\mathbf{u}$, $\eta$, AB history); on the \textbf{changed nodes} it substitutes \emph{patched} values.
The two regions are mutually incompatible, and the \textbf{seam between them} is the instability.
Zeroing the dynamics globally removes the crash (the ocean re-derives its own balanced flow from
rest) --- which is exactly why all five fill strategies failed: each varied the \emph{patch} while
leaving the \emph{seam}. Zero-dynamics is not a production fix: discarding the ocean's circulation
every leg is unacceptable.

\section{The real increment test (2026-07-13)}\label{sec:increment}

ran its 10 years, and the remap was finally asked to carry \emph{a year of spun-up ocean dynamics

\subsection{This is not good news}\label{sec:sober}

It is tempting to read the numbers optimistically: only 17 of 1399 fully-opened nodes rang; the ocean
absorbed $\sim$99\%; the CFLz transient peaked and \emph{decayed}. All true, and it does mean the
remap machinery is performing better than a ``crashed at day 15'' headline suggests.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{But the honest reading is negative.} The run still crashed. What we have learned is that
\textbf{our ocean reacts violently to ice-shelf and cavity changes mid-run}: a handful of columns
where the ice vanishes outright is enough to kill a leg. A coupled setup in which the ice sheet is
\emph{allowed} to change its cavity every 10 years cannot be one leg's worth of extreme nodes away
from failure. \textbf{Absorbing 99\% of the change is not a passing grade when the remaining 1\% is
fatal.}

%======================================================================
\section{Where to go from here}\label{sec:forward}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Find out why PISM is collapsing, and stop it.} A run shedding 24\% of its floating area
      in a decade is the anomaly, and everything downstream inherits it. \textbf{The first thing to
      check is the sub-shelf melt \emph{we} feed PISM}: if our ocean hands it unrealistic melt, then
      PISM's collapse is our own doing, and the loop closes.

\item \textbf{Try to stabilise the ocean locally.} Reduce the ocean timestep and/or raise viscosity
      \emph{in the neighbourhood of the changed columns}, and see whether the transient can be damped
      enough to survive. A mitigation, not a cure --- but it may keep the coupling running while (1)
      is sorted out. Note the \emph{global} knobs are already exhausted: a global timestep cut does
      \textbf{not} help (60\,s merely swaps the CFLz symptom for an $\eta_n$ one).

\item \textbf{The lasting way forward: compute physically consistent fields for the newly opened
      cavity.} Patching such a column with plausible \emph{values} is what we have been doing, and it
      is exactly what does not work (five fill strategies, all dead; \S\ref{sec:seam}). What is needed
      is a state for the newly opened water that is \textbf{dynamically consistent with its
      surroundings}, not merely a sensible number in each cell. That is the real problem, and the one
      worth solving.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
A per-leg cap on $|\Delta\mathrm{ulev}|$ (defer the extreme tail across legs) remains available as a
blunt robustness guard, but it papers over (1) rather than fixing anything.

\section{Bugs found and fixed along the way}

\subsection{In the remap}

\textbf{Unbounded extrapolation below the old seabed} (esm\_tools \code{eef4f6db}). The first attempt
at the increment leg died at \textbf{mstep 1}: the remapped restart contained $-45.02^\circ$C, a value
present \emph{nowhere} in the source (wet range $[-2.32, 41.72]$, dry cells $0.0$). The remap
\emph{manufactured} it. The column shows the mechanism:
$1.22, -0.99, -3.19, -5.39, -8.69, -13.09, -18.6, -25.2, -34.01, \mathbf{-45.02}$ --- smooth and
accelerating. For columns that get \emph{deeper}, the remap fitted a straight line through the last
two old levels and continued it downward with no clamp. Fixed by taking such water from the nearest
old node actually wet at that depth. \textbf{Never hit before} because every previous remap went
mother\,$\to$\,submesh (a strict \emph{subset}); ice \emph{retreating} grows the mesh and exercises
this path for the first time.

\medskip\noindent
Also: sub-ice \code{ssh}/\code{hbar} zeroing (\code{b17fb5c8}); a \textbf{size guard} so the remap
refuses a restart that does not match the old mesh, instead of silently writing garbage
(\code{3b44a704}).

\subsection{In the coupling plumbing}

Making chunk~1 a \emph{submesh} leg pushed a submesh leg through \code{couple\_out} for the first
time. Every previous run either ran chunk~1 on the \emph{full mesh} (where these paths are trivially
correct) or died early in chunk~3 (before reaching the end of a leg). That exposed:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{OASIS \code{GSSPOS} abort at $\sim$day 300.} Its conservation correction is built from
      the summed source/target ratio; when the summed target residual is $\approx 0$ while the source
      residual is small-but-nonzero, it explodes. Only the heat-to-ice flux (\code{A\_Q\_ice
      $\to$ heat\_ico}) used it. Fixed: new \code{gauswgt\_gsmart} transform (\code{3e94d4da}).
\item \textbf{The namcouple \code{feom} dimension had NEVER been patched, on any leg.}
      \code{fix\_namcouple\_feom\_dim} read \code{\$\{MAXMESH\_DIR\_fesom\}}, which is assigned
      \emph{inside} \code{build\_submesh} --- a different subjob's shell. Always unset $\Rightarrow$
      guard refused $\Rightarrow$ silent no-op. Fixed (\code{3cf29e9e}).
\item \textbf{\code{fesom2ice} loaded the static full mesh} while FESOM's output is on the submesh
      (broadcast error) (\code{63cfcc91}).
\item \textbf{\code{previous\_submesh} is read but never created} --- anywhere. Every leg from chunk~5
      on would have remapped against the wrong mesh (\code{3b44a704}).
\item \textbf{A failed \code{couple\_in} did not stop the workflow.} The coupling function is
      backgrounded, the observe job is not its parent (so it can never see the exit code), and
      \code{observe.py} returns an \emph{empty} error list for every non-compute job. The next leg
      started on an un-remapped restart. Fixed with a \code{.coupling\_failed} sentinel
      (\code{3b44a704}).
\end{itemize}

\noindent
\textbf{The pattern:} the guards were right, and the plumbing around them defeated them.
\code{remap\_oasis\_restart} correctly refused a mis-sized restart --- and the run proceeded anyway.

\section{Method note}

Three root causes were asserted with more confidence than the evidence supported, and all three were
later falsified by a control that was cheap and should have come \emph{first}:

\begin{enumerate}
\item ``geostrophic imbalance'' --- killed by the \textbf{cold start} (is it the geometry or our
      state?);
\item ``the sub-ice ssh BC'' --- killed by simply \textbf{running the fix};
\item ``the increment is gentle ($\approx$22\,m)'' --- killed by \textbf{plotting the distribution}
      instead of trusting a mean over a domain that is 98\% unchanged.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
The third is the sharpest: the 22\,m figure was computed correctly and reported honestly, and it was
still misleading enough to steer the strategy for a day. \textbf{Two figures did in ten minutes what
a summary statistic obscured for a day.}
